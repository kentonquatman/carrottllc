  • Exelon Corporation

    Exelon is among the largest utilities in the United States thanks to its merger with Constellation Energy.

    Líders' involvement with Exelon began with a conversation with John Rowe, then the chairman and CEO of Exelon. The topic was a succession plan for a larger, more diverse utility. Mr. Rowe saw the opportunity to create value for shareholders through a merger or acquisition, and at the time, neither he nor the board saw clear internal succession possibilities.

    Exelon identified eight potential acquisitions, including Constellation, and a dozen of the companies’ executives, and asked Líders to assess the executives as candidates to become chief executive of a merged company. Líders prepared reports based upon firsthand knowledge of the "candidates," third-party research and references. The references included former employees and directors, bankers, analysts and regulators. The number of executives expanded over time as Exelon considered more options. No confidence was ever breeched.

    Christopher Crane from Exelon became the chief executive of Exelon with the merger, and Constellation’s chairman and chief executive Mayo Shattuck became the chairman of the Exelon board.

  • Nash Finch Company

    Nash Finch was the second largest publicly traded grocery wholesaler even before its merger with Spartan Stores last November. Ron Marshall had been its CEO for seven years when he announced his resignation. Líders had placed him.

    The board retained Líders, again, to conduct the search for a new CEO. As Líders began to interview directors, however, the board split over corporate direction. Half favored continued acquisitions; half favored operational excellence and no more acquisitions. Líders facilitated the discussions in which the board reached consensus around operational excellence as the most likely avenue for developing shareholder value. This led to the recruitment of Alec Covington as CEO through Líders.

    Líders recruited five directors to the board of Nash Finch, including, most recently, Chris Bodine, the retired executive vice-president of marketing and merchandising at CVS Caremark. In addition, Líders conducted two consulting engagements for the board.

    I don't care that you don't have three million names in a database somewhere. I do care that you have access to the three dozen who matter to us and have an objective point of view.

    William R. Voss

    Former Board Chairman of Nash Finch & Director of SpartanNash

  • Snap-on Incorporated

    Snap-on may best be known for its iconic hand tools, but the company has greatly changed its position in the marketplace through acquisitions that make lifts and diagnostic equipment for vehicle-repair shops, and software for managing parts and repair shops themselves.

    When the CEO who had overseen the acquisitions retired, the board conducted an ill-fated search. The new CEO lasted only a few years before the board terminated him, and board member Jack Michaels, who had just retired as the CEO of HNI Corporation, stepped into the role.

    Mr. Michaels brought order and discipline to Snap-on and began the development of possible successors. When he announced his retirement, however, the board still had questions about internal candidates. It launched another search, this time using Líders.

    Líders presented four external candidates, one from the U.K. and two who have since become the CEOs of other public companies, along with assessing the three internal candidates. The search quickly became due diligence for the board as an internal candidate, Nick Pinchuk, emerged and became COO, then CEO.

    Mr. Pinchuk has re-jiggered Snap-on’s franchise business, triggering growth in the United States and overseas. He has linked the acquisitions into integrated offerings for car-repair shops and dealerships. Profits and cash flow have increased, and the shares of Snap-on have almost tripled since Mr. Pinchuk became CEO.

    Líders subsequently recruited Jim Holden, the former president-CEO of DaimlerChrysler Corporation, and Gregg Sherrill, chairman and CEO of Tenneco Incorporated, to the Snap-on board.

The Líders Team

Gregory T. Carrott

Chicago

Greg Carrott has worked in executive search for 25 years. His engagements include finding dozens of chief executive officers, including for Argonne National Laboratory, Brady Corporation, Cott Bottling, Federal Signal Corporation, Molson and Ontario Power Generation.

His years in search have included seven years at Heidrick & Struggles and 13 years at Egon Zehnder International. He came to search with significant management experience. He was a staff officer of Borg-Warner Corporation and a vice-president and management supervisor of a division of Young & Rubicam. Earlier, he worked for Chicago's Continental Bank and Northwest Industries Incorporated.

Carrott received a B.A. from Lawrence University, an M.S.J. from Northwestern University's Medill School of Journalism and an M.B.A. from the The University of Chicago. He served for five years on an advisory board of the strategy-consulting firm L.E.K. Consulting LLC and has written articles for The Corporate Board, Directorship, Directors & Boards and the Harvard Business Review.

Dr. Thomas Kell

Hamburg

Thomas Kell lead the management appraisal practice of Heidrick & Struggles in Europe and in Asia. He was with Heidrick & Struggles from 1996 until 2012, becoming a senior partner in 2002.
